1

Verify It's a Real Factory, Not a Trading Company

This is the #1 most important step. Trading companies mark up prices by 20-50% and have no control over production quality. Here's how to tell the difference:

  • Factory address matches industrial zone, not commercial building
  • Business license shows "manufacturer" or "factory" in name
  • Willing to conduct video factory tour (live or pre-recorded)
  • Owns production equipment (looms, printing machines, cutting machines)
  • Employs production workers (ask for worker count vs. total staff)
💡 Ask for a video walkthrough of the factory floor. A genuine manufacturer will be happy to show you their production lines, warehouse, and quality control area. If a supplier refuses or makes excuses, that's a major red flag.
2

Check Production Capacity & Equipment

Before placing a large order, confirm the factory can actually handle your volume. Ask about:

  • Daily production capacity (Smith Ribbon: 100,000+ meters/day)
  • Number of production lines — multiple lines mean redundancy if one breaks down
  • Machine types — modern air-jet and rapier looms for better quality
  • Equipment brands — leading brands like Picanol, Dobby indicate better investment
  • Lead time under full load — can they still deliver on time during peak season?

4

Examine Quality Control Processes

Poor QC means defective products, costly returns, and damaged reputation. A reputable manufacturer should have:

  • Documented QC procedures at every production stage
  • In-line inspections during weaving, dyeing, printing, and packaging
  • AQL (Acceptable Quality Limit) standard defined — typically AQL 2.5 for textiles
  • Final random inspection before shipment
  • Inspection report with photos and measurement data
  • Clear defect classification: critical, major, minor

5

Compare Pricing Without Compromising Quality

Price is important, but the lowest price usually means lowest quality. Here's how to evaluate pricing fairly:

  • Compare unit price per meter, not total order price
  • Get quotes from 3-5 factories with identical specifications
  • Ask for FOB, CIF, and DDP pricing to compare apples-to-apples
  • Beware of prices 30%+ below market average — something is usually wrong
  • Negotiate based on annual volume — bigger commitment = better pricing
  • Factor in hidden costs: sample fees, design fees, express shipping, inspection costs
6

Test Communication Responsiveness

How a supplier communicates before you place an order is exactly how they'll communicate after. Test for:

  • Response time — reputable factories respond within 24 hours
  • Clarity — do they answer your specific questions or send generic responses?
  • Proactiveness — do they anticipate problems and offer solutions?
  • Language quality — clear English indicates professional management
  • Business email domain — serious companies use domain emails, not free services
  • Responses take more than 48 hours consistently
  • Default to WeChat/WhatsApp for all communications (harder to track)
  • Grammar and spelling errors in every message
7

Request & Evaluate Samples

Never place a bulk order without first approving physical samples. When evaluating samples:

  • Color accuracy — does it match your specification or Pantone code?
  • Weave quality — no loose threads, even weave density
  • Print clarity — sharp edges on printed logos/designs
  • Color fastness — wash test (if applicable) to check dye bleeding
  • Feel and drape — does the texture match your product requirements?
  • Width tolerance — measure to confirm within ±3% of spec
💡 Pro Tip: Order samples in your actual order quantities. If ordering 3,000m of red satin ribbon, order a 50m sample in the same red. Dye lots vary between production runs, so a sample from a different batch may not match the bulk order.
8

Check References & Client Portfolio

A factory with a strong track record speaks for itself. Ask for:

  • Client references — contact info for 2-3 current or past buyers
  • Client portfolio — major brands they currently supply
  • Export countries — experience with your target market's standards
  • Years in business — longevity indicates stability (Smith Ribbon: 20+ years)
  • Trade show participation — Canton Fair, Atlanta Gift Show, Nuremberg Spielwarenmesse
