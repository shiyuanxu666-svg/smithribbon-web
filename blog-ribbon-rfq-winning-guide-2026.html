How to Write a Winning RFQ for Custom Printed Ribbons — The Brand Buyer's Field Guide 2026

📅 Published: May 9, 2026 ✍️ Smith Ribbon Procurement Team 📖 8 min read

Why Most Ribbon RFQs Fall Apart Before the Quote Arrives

You send a request for quote. You wait three days. The supplier responds with a price — but it's for the wrong width, the wrong material, and assumes a 5,000-meter order when you need 800. You go back and forth for two weeks, exchange twelve emails, and still don't have a clear number.

This is the most common complaint from brand buyers new to sourcing custom printed ribbons from China. And almost every time, the problem isn't the manufacturer — it's the RFQ itself.

A vague request gets a vague quote. An imprecise brief produces an imprecise product. The good news: writing a winning ribbon RFQ is a learnable skill. This guide gives you the exact structure to get accurate, comparable, actionable quotes in a single submission.

💡 Key Principle: Think of your RFQ as a product specification sheet, not just a price inquiry. The more complete your brief, the more accurate your quote — and the fewer surprises in production.

The 7 Must-Have Sections of a Ribbon RFQ

1. Product Description: Material, Type & Construction

Specify the ribbon base material first — this is the single most important variable affecting price and lead time. Common options:

  • Polyester satin — Most popular, cost-effective, wide color range
  • Grosgrain polyester — Textured, durable, ideal for retail packaging
  • Organza / sheer — Decorative, lightweight, translucent
  • Velvet / microfiber — Premium feel, luxury brands
  • Jacquard woven — Pattern woven in, no print needed, highest durability

Include whether you need single-sided or double-sided printing, and whether the ribbon should be wired-edge or non-wired.

2. Dimensions: Width, Length & Thickness

Width tolerance matters more than most buyers realize. A ribbon specified as "3/8 inch" can range from 9mm to 11mm depending on whether the manufacturer uses metric or imperial conversion. Be explicit:

Example: Width: 25mm (±0.5mm), Thickness: 0.18mm, Roll length: 50 meters per roll (or: continuous length with cut-to-size option)

Also specify core size (commonly 1 inch or 3 inch) for compatibility with your dispensers.

3. Print Specification: Artwork, Colors & Color Matching

This is where most RFQs lose precision. Include all of the following:

  • Artwork format: AI or EPS vector files are required for print production. PDFs are acceptable for reference only — not for plate-making.
  • Color system: Specify Pantone (PMS) codes for each color. Do not say "navy blue" — Pantone 281 C and Pantone 2736 C are both navy but print very differently.
  • Print method: Rotary printing (cost-effective for large runs), flexographic, or heat transfer — each produces different color saturation and detail resolution.
  • Color matching: Request a lab dip or strike-off sample before bulk production. Delta E (ΔE) tolerance of ≤3.0 is standard for most brands; luxury buyers typically require ≤2.0.
⚠️ Common Mistake: Sending a JPG mockup and expecting the manufacturer to "figure out" the Pantone colors. A JPG cannot reliably communicate exact colors. Always provide physical or digital Pantone references alongside your artwork.

4. Quantity & MOQ Clarity

State your anticipated order quantity honestly — and ask the supplier to quote on multiple quantity tiers. This reveals the real cost curve and helps with volume planning.

Also clarify whether the MOQ applies per design or per colorway — some manufacturers combine these, which can inflate your minimum commitment significantly.

5. Quality Standards & Testing Requirements

Define your acceptance criteria upfront. At minimum, request:

  • Color consistency: Delta E ≤3.0 between strike-off and bulk production
  • Print registration tolerance: ±0.5mm for multi-color designs
  • Physical testing: Rub fastness (crocking), wash resistance if applicable, light fastness (ISO 105-B02 minimum Class 4)
  • Pre-shipment inspection: Specify AQL level (typically 2.5 for general products, 1.5 for luxury)

6. Packaging & Labeling Requirements

Packaging is often overlooked in RFQs but has real cost implications. Specify:

  • Individual roll wrapping (plastic bag, cardboard disc, or paper core)
  • Carton dimensions and weight limits for your freight carrier
  • Labeling: factory label only, or your own brand label with barcodes/SKUs
  • Palletization requirements for FCL (full container load) shipments

7. Target Price, Incoterm & Delivery Timeline

Suppliers quote differently depending on whether you want FOB, CIF, or DDP pricing. State your preferred incoterm and target destination upfront. Also communicate your production timeline requirements:

  • Do you need samples before a production decision?
  • Is this for a seasonal launch with a hard launch date?
  • What is your acceptable lead time range (standard: 14–21 days for print production + 5–7 days shipping)?
💡 Pro Tip: Share your budget range privately after receiving initial quotes. This gives reputable manufacturers room to find creative cost solutions rather than simply declining due to perceived lowball pricing.

RFQ Template: Copy and Customize

Use this structure for your next custom printed ribbon RFQ:

SUBJECT: Custom Printed Ribbon RFQ — [Your Brand / Product Line]

1. Material: [e.g., Polyester satin, single-sided]
2. Dimensions: [Width × Thickness × Roll Length]
3. Print Design: [Describe or attach AI/EPS artwork]
4. Colors: [Pantone codes for each color — minimum 1 reference swatch]
5. Print Method: [Rotary / Flexo / Heat transfer — or "Please recommend"]
6. Quantity Tiers: [500m / 1,000m / 5,000m / 10,000m]
7. MOQ Clarification: [Per design? Per colorway?]
8. Quality Standards: [ΔE tolerance, AQL level, test requirements]
9. Packaging: [Roll wrapping, carton, labeling requirements]
10. Incoterm: [FOB Xiamen / CIF [Port] / DDP]
11. Destination: [Country / Port]
12. Target Lead Time: [By when?]
13. Target Budget: [Optional — per meter or total]
14. Sample Required: [Yes / No — if yes, qty needed]

What to Do When You Receive Quotes

A quote is not just a number — it's information. Compare quotes across these dimensions, not just unit price:

  • Included vs. excluded costs: Does the quote include cylinder/plate fees, sample costs, and packaging?
  • Payment terms: 30% deposit / 70% balance vs. L/C at sight have significant cost implications
  • Sample lead time: How long until you receive a strike-off for approval?
  • Communication quality: Did the supplier ask clarifying questions? Or just send a price?

The suppliers who ask detailed questions before quoting are the same ones who will manage your order professionally. Price-only comparisons often lead to price-only disappointments.

⚠️ Red Flag: If a supplier quotes without asking about Pantone colors, artwork format, or quantity tiers, they are guessing. That guess will be reflected in the final invoice.
