2. Artwork File Requirements: The Non-Negotiables

Before you send anything to a factory, your artwork files must meet these baseline requirements. Think of them as the grammar of your communication — without correct grammar, even the best ideas get lost in translation.

Accepted File Formats (Ranked by Preference)

  • Vector files (.AI, .EPS, .PDF): Preferred. Can be scaled infinitely without loss of quality. Essential for woven and screen-printed ribbons where fine details and text are involved.
  • High-resolution raster (.PNG, .PSD, .TIFF): Acceptable for simple logos only. Minimum 300 DPI at actual print size. Logos with fine text or gradients below 300 DPI will appear pixelated on narrow ribbons.
  • JPEG and low-res files: Generally not acceptable for production. Only use for preliminary concept review, never for final approval.

Color Mode

Always work in CMYK color mode for printed ribbons or Pantone (Spot Colors) for precise brand color matching. RGB files submitted for production will be converted, and the factory is not responsible for color shifts that result from mode conversion.

Artwork Clearances and Bleeds

Your logo must be placed with adequate clear space (minimum 3mm on all sides within the print area). If your design includes a full-bleed background color, extend it at least 3mm beyond the trim edge to account for cutting tolerance.

Text Handling

All text in production artwork must be either outlined (converted to paths) or supplied with all fonts embedded. If you send an AI file with live text but no fonts, the factory will substitute — and the result is rarely what you intended.

3. Color Matching: From Pantone to Factory Output

Color consistency is one of the top two sources of brand buyer frustration (the other being dimension tolerance). Understanding how Chinese factories match color will help you set realistic expectations and get what you specified.

The Pantone System Explained

Pantone (now part of X-Rite) is the global standard for precise color communication. When you specify "Pantone 185 C" for your ribbon, you are giving the factory an exact target. A competent factory will use a Pantone color guide to mix pigments and confirm the match visually before running the full production order.

For printed ribbons: CMYK values can produce reasonable approximations but will not be identical to your brand's Pantone. Always state your Pantone number as the authority, with CMYK values as secondary reference.

For woven ribbons: Color matching is done by yarn dye lots. This is inherently less precise than printed color. Specify your Pantone and ask the factory to provide a pre-production swatch (typically 3–5 business days) for your approval before bulk production.

Pro Tip: Always request a physical pre-production sample (PPS) or dye-lot lab dip before committing to bulk production for any order where color accuracy is critical to your brand standard. Budget 7–14 days and $30–$150 USD for this step depending on order complexity.

4. Material & Weave Specifications

Material choice is where many brand buyers under-specify, leading to ribbons that look right in the sample but fail in the field. Your spec sheet must address these material variables:

Base Ribbon Type

  • Satin: Smooth, high-sheen surface. Ideal for luxury packaging, gift wrapping, and apparel labels.
  • Grosgrain: Textured, matte weave with excellent durability. Preferred for apparel labels, bags, and heavy-duty packaging.
  • Organza: Sheer, lightweight, and decorative. Not suitable for load-bearing applications.
  • Velvet: Soft, plush texture. Premium feel for holiday, home décor, and luxury gifting.
  • Jacquard: Woven patterns with inherent texture (not printed). The gold standard for logo labels in fashion.
  • Polyester: Durable, colorfast, and cost-effective. Preferred for outdoor and washable applications.

Width and Length

Specify width in millimeters (mm). Industry-standard widths range from 5mm (¾ inch) to 100mm (4 inches) for most applications. State the width tolerance your brand can accept — typically ±2mm for wide ribbons and ±1mm for narrow ribbons under 15mm.

Weight (GSM)

Grams per square meter (GSM) affects the ribbon's feel and drape. A heavier GSM (e.g., 150–200 gsm) gives a premium hand-feel; lighter GSM (80–120) is more economical and suitable for high-volume, cost-sensitive applications.

7. The 7 Most Costly Mistakes Brand Buyers Make

⚠️ Errors That Lead to Reorders and Delays

  1. Sending RGB instead of CMYK or Pantone: Colors will shift. Always submit in the correct color mode.
  2. Not requesting a pre-production sample: The sample is your last chance to correct before bulk production starts.
  3. Assuming MOQ is negotiable without asking: MOQ policies vary. Confirm before designing around a quantity that may not be producible.
  4. Ignoring dye-lot variation: Different production batches of the same yarn can vary slightly in color. Order all needed ribbon in one batch.
  5. Specifying "close to Pantone X" instead of exact: "Close" is subjective. Exact Pantone numbers eliminate ambiguity.
  6. Forgetting export packaging requirements: If your order crosses international borders, specify export-standard packaging to prevent transit damage.
  7. Not testing wash/fade resistance: If the ribbons will be used on washable products (apparel labels, pet accessories), specify colorfastness requirements.
