Width Tolerance Standards by Ribbon Type

Manufacturing tolerances define the acceptable range of deviation from your specified width. These tolerances are influenced by the ribbon's construction (woven vs. printed), material (polyester vs. satin vs. grosgrain), and production method.

Ribbon TypeStandard TolerancePremium Tolerance (+cost)Notes
Woven Satin±2mm±1mmTighter tolerance achievable with loom recalibration
Polyester Satin±3mm±1.5mmStretch during finishing affects tolerance
Grosgrain±2mm±1mmRibbed texture can complicate measurement
Jacquard Woven±2mm±1mmPattern complexity does not affect width tolerance
Printed Ribbon±2mm±1mmSubstrate stretch during printing is primary variable
Velvet Ribbon±3mm±1.5mmPile height adds variability to measured width
Wire-Edged Ribbon±3mm±2mmWire adds structural variability at edges
Important: When specifying tolerance, always reference it in writing on your Purchase Order. A verbal agreement to "be as accurate as possible" is not a commercial specification. Written tolerance = enforceable quality standard.

How Factories Actually Measure Ribbon Width

Understanding the measurement process helps you specify dimensions that match what you'll actually receive. There are two standard methods:

Method 1: Flat Width Measurement

Measured when the ribbon is laid flat on a smooth surface without tension. This is the most common method used for specification sheets and is typically what buyers mean when they specify "width."

Method 2: Edge-to-Edge Measurement (Including Selvage)

Some specifications — particularly for printed ribbons where the print must be precisely positioned — require measurement that includes the unprinted selvage edge. Always clarify which method your specification uses.

Measurement Environment Matters

Temperature and humidity affect textile measurements. Most factories measure in a controlled environment (20°C, 65% RH). If your receiving location has significantly different conditions, allow for ±0.5mm drift per meter as the ribbon acclimates to your environment.

Ordering Custom Widths: What to Include in Your RFQ

When you need a non-standard width, the completeness of your specification determines how quickly and accurately your supplier can respond. A professional custom width RFQ should include:

  • Nominal width in mm (e.g., 42mm, not "about 1.7 inches")
  • Acceptable tolerance range (e.g., "±1.5mm")
  • Measurement method ("flat width" or "edge-to-edge")
  • Reference sample (physical sample preferred, high-res photo minimum)
  • Material / construction type (satin, grosgrain, jacquard, etc.)
  • Minimum order quantity you're prepared to commit to
  • Application context (this helps the factory suggest the most efficient standard-width alternative if applicable)

The 7 Specification Errors That Cost Buyers the Most

Having reviewed thousands of ribbon RFQs, these are the errors that most frequently lead to wrong production runs, disputes, and wasted lead time:

  1. Converting inches to mm by rounding. 1 inch = 25.4mm exactly. Using 25mm instead of 25.4mm in a 10,000-unit order can mean 400mm of total width deviation — enough to change how the ribbon fits your packaging.
  2. Not specifying tolerance. Without a tolerance, the factory is only obligated to "try their best." Specify a range so both parties know what is acceptable.
  3. Confusing width with length. Always state "width" explicitly. "Size" is ambiguous in textile contexts.
  4. Assuming the selvage edge is included. The printed or colored area is narrower than the full roll width. Always clarify whether your width specification refers to the visible area or the full material width.
  5. Not requesting pre-production samples. For custom widths, always order a sample run (even 50–100m) before committing to full production. The sample confirms the width looks and performs correctly in your application.
  6. Ignoring roll core size. Ribbon roll core diameter affects how the ribbon dispenses in your machinery. Standard cores are 1", 2", or 3" (25mm, 50mm, 76mm). Specify this if you're loading ribbon into automated equipment.
  7. Not checking dye lot consistency. Width tolerances assume consistent substrate. If color matching across reorders is important, specify a single dye lot for the full quantity, or accept ±0.5 Delta E color variation across rolls.
