In a typical ribbon OEM project, the sample approval stage takes 3–6 weeks. In our experience at Smith Ribbon, approximately 70% of the delays in that window come from artwork file issues — not from the factory. Brands submit files that are not print-ready, the factory flags them, the brand sends revisions, and the cycle repeats.

Each cycle adds 5–10 business days to the timeline. For a brand preparing for a seasonal launch, one artwork rejection cycle can mean missing the shipping window entirely.

This guide catalogs the 10 most common artwork file mistakes that cause ribbon production delays. Procurement teams can use this as a pre-submission checklist to ensure files are clean the first time.

Why Artwork Files Are the #1 Cause of Ribbon Production Delays

Ribbon printing is fundamentally different from sheet-fed paper printing. Ribbons are a continuous substrate that passes through a printing cylinder at high speed. The design repeat (called the "pitch" or "repeat length") must align precisely with the cylinder circumference. If the artwork file is not set up correctly for this continuous printing process, the factory cannot proceed — regardless of how good the design looks on a PDF proof.

The consequences of artwork rejection cascade quickly: delayed sample approval, compressed mass production timeline, potential air freight costs to meet delivery deadlines, and in the worst cases, a brand forced to launch with blank ribbon instead of branded ribbon.

Cost of One Artwork Rejection Cycle: Typically 7–14 days added to the project timeline. At a daily cost of $500–$2,000 in extended样品 (sample) fees, logistics adjustments, and internal team time, a single artwork rejection can cost $5,000–$20,000 in hidden project costs — before the ribbon is even printed.
MISTAKE #1Submitting RGB Instead of CMYK

The Problem

Most design software defaults to RGB color mode. Brand designers create artwork in RGB, view it on RGB monitors, and submit RGB files to the ribbon factory. The factory prints in CMYK (or spot/Pantone colors). RGB colors that look vibrant on screen often convert to muted, flat, or completely unexpected colors when translated to CMYK — particularly bright blues, neon greens, and hot pinks.

How to Fix It

Fix: Set your design software to CMYK color mode before starting the artwork. Convert any embedded images to CMYK. If the design uses brand Pantone spot colors, those must be specified as Pantone references (e.g., Pantone 186 C) — not as RGB approximations — and the file must document which Pantone numbers were used.
MISTAKE #2No Bleed Area on Repeating Patterns

The Problem

Ribbon designs with repeating patterns — chevrons, stripes, polka dots, all-over logos — require bleed. The printing cylinder needs extra pattern repeats beyond the intended cut length so the factory can trim the ribbon cleanly at the exact specified length without leaving unprinted edges. Without bleed, every cut length will have a blank edge.

How to Fix It

Fix: Always extend the artwork repeat by a minimum of 5% on each end of the design. For example, if you need10,000 meters of ribbon with a 30 cm repeat, provide artwork files covering at least 31.5 cm per repeat — with the extra1.5 cm being an identical continuation of the pattern, not a white gap. Label the file clearly with the intended repeat length vs the total file length.
MISTAKE #3Low-Resolution Artwork (<300 DPI)

The Problem

Images embedded in ribbon artwork files are frequently sourced from websites, presentations, or low-resolution stock libraries at 72–150 DPI. At those resolutions, fine details — small text, thin logo lines, halftone patterns — will print as blurs or artifacts on the narrow ribbon surface. On grosgrain or satin ribbon with a textured weave, the resolution problem is amplified.

How to Fix It

Fix: All raster images in ribbon artwork must be provided at a minimum of 300 DPI at the actual print size — for a 25 mm wide ribbon, that means images must be at least 300 DPI when rendered at 25 mm wide. For fine detail work (small text below 3mm, thin lines below 0.5mm), consider using vector artwork (AI/EPS format) instead of raster images.
MISTAKE #4Using Linked vs Embedded Images

The Problem

Designers often work with linked (not embedded) image files in Adobe Illustrator or InDesign. When the packaged file is submitted to the factory, the linked images may not be included — or they may be the wrong version. The factory receives the layout file without the actual image assets, and the printed result will be missing design elements entirely.

How to Fix It

Fix: Always use the "Package" or "Collect for Output" function in your design software before submitting. This bundles all linked files into a single folder. Provide both the packaged folder AND a high-resolution PDF export of the artwork as a visual reference. Require the factory to confirm all linked assets are present before confirming the pre-press stage.
MISTAKE #5Missing Pantone (Spot Color) References

The Problem

When a brand specifies "navy blue," the factory needs to know exactly which navy. Without a Pantone reference (e.g., Pantone 2767 C), the factory will interpret "navy" from a CMYK build — and the result will vary between production batches. For brands with strict color consistency requirements (cosmetics, luxury retail), this variation is unacceptable.

How to Fix It

Fix: Provide a physical Pantone chip or digital Pantone swatch book reference for every spot color in the design. List each color with its Pantone number in a separate color callout legend on the artwork file. For digital proofing, use Adobe Illustrator's Pantone color system and include the Pantone number in the color name field. Note: screen-printed ribbon inks cannot perfectly match all Pantone colors — ask your factory to provide a printed color swatch on the actual ribbon substrate before mass production.
MISTAKE #6Editable Text Instead of Outlined Text

The Problem

Text in artwork files is only editable if the original fonts are installed on the factory's computer. When fonts are missing, the factory's pre-press software will substitute a default font — changing the typeface, kerning, and possibly the text itself. A logo tagline that says "Pure Elegance" can become "Purc Elcgzncc" with a missing font substitution.

How to Fix It

Fix: Either (a) outline all text in Adobe Illustrator using Type > Create Outlines before submitting, or (b) include all font files used in the artwork alongside the packaged submission. Outlining is the safer and more standard approach for ribbon printing. Note: once outlined, text cannot be edited — review carefully before outlining.
MISTAKE #7Wrong File Format for the Print Method

The Problem

Ribbon printing methods include rotary screen printing, flatbed screen printing, flexographic printing, sublimation/dye-sublimation, and hot stamping. Each method requires different file preparation. A file prepared for rotary screen printing (which uses a cylinder) has different requirements than one for flatbed screen. Submitting a flatbed-prepared file to a rotary printer will cause alignment problems.

How to Fix It

Fix: Confirm the print method with the factory before preparing artwork. Request the factory's artwork specification document (art spec sheet), which will specify: required file format (AI, EPS, PDF), color mode (CMYK, spot, or both), bleed requirements, template file with die lines, and any specific overprint or trapping settings. Do not assume your standard brand template is print-method ready without this confirmation.
MISTAKE #8Improper Color Separation in Multi-Color Designs

The Problem

Multi-color ribbon designs require color separation — each color printed as a separate layer (channel) in the artwork file. Designers sometimes submit artwork with all colors on a single layer, or with color separations that don't align correctly. The factory must then recreate the separations, which introduces the risk of misalignment and color registration errors on the printed ribbon.

How to Fix It

Fix: Provide artwork with each spot color as a separate, named layer in the AI/EPS file. For CMYK process printing, provide artwork with clean channel separation. Include a composite color proof showing the final printed appearance, and separate layer proofs showing each individual color channel. Include a color separation guide that names each layer and its corresponding Pantone reference.
MISTAKE #9Not Confirming Color Matching Across Print Batches

The Problem

Even with perfect initial artwork, ribbon color can shift between production batches. Ink lot changes, printing machine calibration differences, ribbon substrate dye lot variations, and ambient humidity on different print days all contribute to batch-to-batch color variation. Brands that approve a sample in February and place a reorder in June may receive ribbon with a noticeably different color — despite using the same artwork file.

How to Fix It

Fix: Establish a color tolerance standard with your factory before the first order. The industry standard for textile ribbon printing is delta-E (dE) < 2.0 for identical Pantone matching. Request that the factory provide a color measurement report (using a spectrophotometer) with each production batch, referencing the original approved sample swatch. Store an approved physical sample swatch in your quality archive as the permanent color reference standard.
MISTAKE #10Ignoring Ribbon Print Repeat (Pitch) Requirements

The Problem

Every ribbon design has a repeat length — the distance from the start of one design unit to the start of the next. This repeat must match the printing cylinder's circumference exactly. If it doesn't, the design will not align across the roll, and the result will show a visible "seam" or mismatch at the cylinder circumference point. This is the most ribbonspecific technical error and the hardest to fix after production has begun.

How to Fix It

Fix: Ask your factory for the exact cylinder circumference (the "pitch" or "repeat length") that will be used for your order before preparing artwork. Design the artwork to fit exactly within that repeat — no more, no less. If the design is not evenly divisible by the repeat length, work with the factory's pre-press team to adjust the layout. Do not assume the design can simply be "cropped" to fit after the file is submitted.

Pre-Submission Artwork Checklist

  1. Color mode set to CMYK (not RGB)
  2. All spot colors specified with Pantone references
  3. All images embedded or packaged with linked files confirmed
  4. All raster images at minimum 300 DPI at print size
  5. All text outlined (or font files included)
  6. Bleed area extended by minimum 5% at each end of repeat
  7. Artwork file format matches the confirmed print method
  8. Each print color on a separate, named layer
  9. Artwork repeat length confirmed with factory pre-press team
  10. Composite PDF proof and separate layer proofs provided
  11. Color callout legend included on artwork file
  12. Factory artwork spec sheet reviewed and followed

Frequently Asked Questions

What file format should I send for ribbon printing?

For rotary screen printing (the most common method for custom ribbon), the preferred format is Adobe Illustrator (AI) or EPS, with all fonts outlined and all images embedded. PDF/X-1a is also acceptable as a visual proof, but the AI/EPS file is required for the printing plates. Always confirm with your factory — some flexographic printers prefer PDF or PSD formats.

Can I use PNG or JPG files for ribbon printing?

PNG and JPG are raster formats with significant limitations for ribbon printing: they are fixed resolution (not scalable without quality loss), have no layer separation capability, and do not carry color profile data reliably. They should only be used as a reference image — not as the production artwork file. If you must submit a raster file, it must be at 600 DPI minimum at print size.

How do I know if my Pantone color can be printed on ribbon?

Not all Pantone colors can be accurately reproduced with ribbon printing inks on all substrates. Metallic Pantones, fluorescent Pantones, and very dark Pantones (especially near-black) are particularly challenging. Ask your factory to provide a printed swatch on the actual ribbon material before you commit to the design. This costs a small sample fee but can prevent a much larger problem.

What is the typical lead time for a ribbon artwork pre-press review?

Most ribbon factories require3–5 business days to review artwork and provide a pre-press report. Complex multi-color designs with multiple Pantone references may require 7–10 days. Build this review period into your project timeline before the sample stage — it is not a "fast track" item that can be skipped to save time.
