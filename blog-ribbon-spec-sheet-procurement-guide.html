How to Read a Ribbon Spec Sheet: A Procurement Manager's Technical Cheat Sheet

The first document your supplier sends you — and the one most buyers gloss over. This guide decodes every field so you can spot red flags before they become production problems.

1. Material Composition: The Foundation

The first line of any spec sheet should tell you exactly what the ribbon is made of — and how much of it. Watch for vague terms like "polyester blend" with no percentages, which can indicate inconsistent raw material sourcing.

⚠️ Red Flag

Specification reads "100% polyester" but no test report or mill certificate is attached. Always request an independent lab report (e.g., SGS or Bureau Veritas) for material composition claims — especially for children's product applications.

Common ribbon materials and what they mean for your procurement:

  • Polyester (PET): Most common base. Strong, colorfast, affordable. Washable and resistant to UV degradation. Best choice for most retail packaging applications.
  • Nylon (PA): Superior tensile strength and elasticity. Often specified for automotive or industrial applications. Higher cost than polyester.
  • Satin (woven polyester or acetate): Identified by weave structure rather than fiber type. Polyester satin is more durable; acetate satin is softer but more prone to water staining.
  • OEKO-TEX® certified materials: Indicates the base fabric has been tested for over 100 harmful substances. Mandatory for most EU and US retail brands in the apparel and home décor space.
  • RPET (Recycled PET): Made from post-consumer plastic bottles. Requires GRS (Global Recycled Standard) certification to claim recycled content on your product labels.

2. Construction: Weave, Denier & Pick Count

These three fields together tell you almost everything about how a ribbon will feel, look, and perform in production.

Denier (D)
Measures linear mass density — the weight of a single filament thread per 9,000 meters. A higher Denier means a thicker, heavier ribbon. For retail packaging ribbons, 75D–150D is typical. For heavy-duty grosgrain or decorative bows, look for 150D–300D.
Pick Count (or Thread Count)
The number of weft threads per inch of fabric. Higher pick counts mean tighter weave, better durability, and sharper print detail. A quality grosgrain ribbon typically has a pick count of 80+ per inch.
Weave Type
Determines texture and performance. Grosgrain has a tightly woven, ribbed surface — ideal for printing logos. Satin has a smooth, glossy face. Jacquard uses colored yarns to create woven patterns without ink — extremely durable for outdoor applications.

3. Dimensions & Tolerance

This is where many buyers skip ahead, but tolerance specs are critical — especially when the ribbon is being die-cut, overlocked, or assembled into a finished bow.

  • Width: Usually stated in millimeters (mm). Common widths: 9mm, 13mm, 16mm, 25mm, 38mm, 50mm, 75mm, 100mm. Check whether the stated width is nominal (approx.) or actual cut width.
  • Tolerance: Most factories operate to ±2mm for width. For tight-assembly applications (e.g., pre-tied bows with snap fasteners), demand ±1mm or confirm by requesting a production sample.
  • Thickness: Usually measured in microns (μm) or mils (1 mil = 0.0254mm). Thicker doesn't always mean better — for thin decorative wraps, a lighter ribbon is preferred.
  • Roll Length: Standard roll length is 50 yards (45.7m) or 100 yards (91.4m). Verify if length is measured cut or uncut, and check for splice joints (a quality factory will not include splices in rolls for retail).

4. Color Matching: Pantone, Delta-E & Systems

Color mismatch is the #1 cause of rework and rejection in custom ribbon orders. Understanding these fields helps you set enforceable standards at the PO stage.

  • Pantone (PMS): The industry-standard color reference system. A spec sheet should reference a specific PMS number (e.g., "PMS 2935 C" for a corporate blue). Without this, "navy blue" means different things to different people — and factories.
  • Delta-E (ΔE): A numerical measure of color difference. ΔE ≤ 1: imperceptible to human eye. ΔE ≤ 3: acceptable for most packaging. ΔE ≤ 5: visible difference. Always specify your maximum acceptable ΔE in the PO — industry standard is ΔE ≤ 3 for premium retail products.
  • CMYK Process: Used for photographic or full-color printed ribbons. Note that CMYK cannot reproduce all Pantone spot colors — always confirm a Pantone-to-CMYK conversion with your supplier before approving proofs.
  • Dye Lot Tolerance: Ribbon dyed in different batches may show subtle shade variation. Specify "same dye lot" if color consistency across multiple rolls is critical to your product.

5. Physical Performance: Tensile, Fray & Shrinkage

These metrics matter most for ribbons used in high-stress applications — automated packaging lines, apparel attachment, or outdoor décor.

Tensile Strength (N / kgf)
Maximum load the ribbon can bear before breaking. Measured in Newtons (N) or kilogram-force (kgf). For retail packaging, 100N–300N is typical. For industrial or heavy-duty use, request 500N+ with test documentation.
Elongation at Break (%)
How much the ribbon stretches before snapping. Low elongation (<20%) = stiff and crisp; higher elongation (>30%) = elastic and forgiving. Important for velvet ribbons used in hair accessories.
Fray Resistance
Factory test: ultrasonic cutting or heat-sealing edges to prevent unraveling. Request fray test results for organza and mesh ribbons specifically — these fray most readily.
Shrinkage Rate (%)
Maximum dimensional change after washing or heat-setting. For ribbons applied to textiles (apparel labels, care tags), shrinkage should be <3%. Request wash test report (ISO 105 C01 or AATCC M301) for textile-adjacent applications.

7. Order-Specific Parameters

  • Core Size: Standard cardboard core is 1.5" (38mm). Plastic cores or custom cardboard diameters may be required for automated dispensing equipment. Specify this upfront — factory cores vary.
  • Winding Direction: Can affect how the ribbon unwinds from the roll. For vertical dispensers, specify "tight wind, no telescoping."
  • Splicing Policy: Quality factories do not splice rolls under 50 yards. Specify "no splices" in your PO if defect-free appearance is critical.
  • Packaging: Individual polybag, header card, or custom box. Specify individually wrapped rolls if retail-ready presentation is required.

8. Certifications & Compliance

Required certifications depend on your target market and product application. Always confirm requirements with your QA team before finalizing a supplier agreement.

  • OEKO-TEX® Standard 100: Mandatory for ribbons used in textile products that contact skin (apparel, baby items, bedding). Tests for harmful substances in the finished ribbon.
  • GRS (Global Recycled Standard): Required if claiming recycled content (RPET). Chain of custody must be documented from raw material to finished product.
  • FSC® (Forest Stewardship Council): Required for paper-based packaging components and hang tags if your brand has FSC certification commitments.
  • REACH / RoHS Compliance: Required for EU market entry. Confirms the ribbon does not contain restricted chemical substances above regulatory thresholds.
  • BSCI / SEDEX / SMETA: Social compliance audits. Increasingly required by major retailers (Walmart, Target, Costco) as part of supplier approval. Smith Ribbon holds all three.
