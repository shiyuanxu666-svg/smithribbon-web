How Ribbon Artwork Pre-Press Works — Color Separation, Print-Ready Files, and Proof Approval for Brand Buyers

Published: May 21, 2026 Reading time: 7 min Smith Ribbon

When a brand sends an artwork file to a ribbon OEM factory, the file doesn't go directly to a printing machine. It passes through a pre-press stage — a sequence of technical steps that convert a design concept into print plates or screens. Misunderstanding this process is one of the leading causes of delayed orders, wasted samples, and unexpected cost overruns in ribbon OEM programs.

This guide explains how the pre-press workflow actually works for ribbon printing, what file specifications to follow, and how to approve proofs without second-guessing the factory every step of the way.

Understanding the Two Main Ribbon Printing Methods

Ribbon printing uses two primary technologies, each with different pre-press requirements:

  • Flexography (flexo): The most common method for large-volume printed ribbons. Uses a flexible photopolymer plate wrapped around a cylinder. Best for simple designs with up to 6 colors. Cost-effective at high volumes (10,000+ meters).
  • Rotary screen printing: Uses a mesh screen with squeegee, suitable for heavier ink deposits and specialty effects (metallic inks, high-build textures). More expensive per print run but handles more complex artwork with finer detail.

Your factory will recommend the most suitable method based on your design complexity, order quantity, and ribbon material. Always confirm the printing method before finalizing your artwork — switching it after proof approval can require starting the pre-press process over.

Key concept: Both flexo and screen printing are spot-color processes — they are not full-color (CMYK) processes like offset or digital printing. If your artwork contains gradients, photographic images, or complex blends, you'll need a different approach (usually simplified to a limited spot-color palette) or the factory may need to print a solid color design instead.

Step 1 — Setting Up Your Print-Ready Artwork File

The most common file format for ribbon OEM artwork is Adobe Illustrator (AI), followed by PDF (print-ready, not web export), CorelDRAW (CDR), or EPS. Always confirm with your factory which format they prefer before starting.

Essential artwork file requirements:

  • Color mode: Spot colors only — specify the exact Pantone color name and number for each color in the design. Do not use CMYK values as a substitute; CMYK is a print process, not a spot color specification, and will yield different results on different machines.
  • Artwork size: Set the document at exactly 1:1 with the intended printed width. If your ribbon is 25mm wide, the artwork should be exactly 25mm wide (or a repeat length dimension matching your order specification).
  • Bleed: For designs that bleed to the edge, extend the artwork 1–2mm beyond the cutting line on each side. Ribbon printing has ±0.5mm tolerance — without bleed, white edge gaps will be visible.
  • Font handling: Convert all text to outlines (vector paths). Never send live text that depends on the factory having the same fonts installed.
  • Stroke and outline: Convert strokes to outlines or expand them so line weights remain consistent. A 0.5pt stroke in a live file may render differently depending on software versions.
  • No embedded images: High-resolution images (300+ DPI) should be linked, not embedded. Confirm that all linked files are included when sending the package.
Best practice: Package your artwork file with a printed proof (PDF) and a separate color swatch note listing the exact Pantone numbers used. Send a layered file — with a visible layers folder and a separate production-ready layer — so the factory's pre-press technician can understand the design structure.

Step 2 — Color Separation

Color separation is the process of taking a multi-color design and generating individual printing plates (for flexo) or screens (for screen printing) for each color. A design with 3 spot colors will require 3 separate plates/screens.

For each color in your artwork, the factory's pre-press team will:

  • Isolate the color as a separate layer
  • Convert it to a halftone or solid fill depending on the design
  • Generate a film positive or CTP (computer-to-plate) output for the press

Complex designs with more than 6 colors will be expensive to print and may require simplification to a 4–5 color palette. If your brand colors require more than 6 spot colors, consider switching to a digital printing method (available for shorter runs of 500–2,000 meters) or simplifying the design.

Pantone Matching on Ribbon Substrates

A critical difference from paper printing: ribbon materials (polyester, grosgrain, satin, velvet) are not white like paper. A Pantone color that looks correct on a printed proof may look noticeably different when printed on a colored or slightly textured ribbon substrate.

Always request a strike-off sample (a short printed length, typically 5–10 meters) on the actual ribbon substrate before the full production run begins. This is the single most effective way to avoid color mismatch at scale.

Common mistake: Approving a color based on a paper proof (a printed sheet of paper simulating the ribbon color) and then finding the production run looks different on the actual polyester ribbon. Always insist on a fabric/strike-off sample on the actual ribbon material, not just a paper proof.

Step 3 — Artwork Proof Approval Workflow

A professional ribbon OEM factory will provide a formal proof approval stage before starting the production run. Here is the standard workflow:

  1. Digital proof: PDF layout showing the design as it will print, with color callouts. This is a flat-color proof — it shows placement and layout, not the actual print color result.
  2. Strike-off sample: A physical printed sample on the actual ribbon, typically 3–5 meters long, showing the real color, texture, and registration. This is your primary approval document.
  3. Approval form: Factory sends an approval form listing color values, repeat length, ribbon width, and any other critical specifications. You must sign and return this before production starts.

Build in 5–7 business days for the proof cycle. Rushing proof approval is the number one cause of errors making it into production runs.

Timeline for a typical ribbon OEM print order:
Artwork submission → Pre-press (3–5 days) → Strike-off sample (3–5 days) → Approval → Production (15–25 days) → QC and shipment (3–5 days)
Total from artwork approval to shipment ready: 25–40 days.

Step 4 — Print Registration and Repeat Length

For continuous ribbon printing, the design must be set up as a repeat pattern — the design repeats at a set interval along the length of the ribbon. The factory sets the print cylinder or screen for the exact repeat length, and the design must be created to align perfectly within that repeat.

If your design has a specific repeat requirement (e.g., a logo needs to appear every 50cm, aligned precisely with the ribbon edge), this must be specified in the purchase order. The factory will calibrate the print machine to match your repeat specification, and this is verified on the strike-off sample.

Repeat length tolerances in professional ribbon printing are typically ±1mm. For designs where visual alignment at the seam is critical (e.g., logo patterns), request a printed sample showing the seam between two repeat units and verify the alignment before approval.

Common Artwork Errors That Cause Production Delays

  • Using CMYK instead of spot colors: The factory will ask you to re-specify the colors as Pantone values, adding 1–3 days to the schedule.
  • Low-resolution images: Images below 300 DPI at the final print size will appear pixelated or blurry when printed at ribbon width. Vector logos are preferred.
  • Missing bleed: Edges of designs will show white gaps after cutting. Requires reprinting or a new proof cycle.
  • Inconsistent stroke weights: Fine lines may drop out during printing. Any stroke under 0.3mm at print width is at risk.
  • Design exceeding print capabilities: Asking for photographic quality or 4-color process printing on a ribbon press will result in a factory rejection or poor quality output. Know your printing method's limitations before designing.
