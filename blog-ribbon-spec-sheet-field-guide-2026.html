How to Read a Ribbon Spec Sheet: A Technical Field Guide for Brand Buyers

📅 Published: May 23, 2026  |  ⏱️ 14 min read  |  🏷️ B2B Technical Guide
Author: Smith Ribbon Technical Team
Updated: May 2026
Target: Brand Buyers, Procurement Teams, Packaging Designers

When your ribbon supplier sends a quotation, it often arrives as a spec sheet dense with codes, numbers, and terminology that can trip up even seasoned procurement managers. A width listed as 25mm ±0.5mm, a weight listed as 68gsm ±5%, a finishing note like Ultrasonic cut, heat-sealed edge — what do these actually mean for your product?

This guide decodes every significant field on a standard ribbon spec sheet so you can evaluate quotes with confidence, spot red flags before production starts, and communicate your exact requirements to any ribbon factory — whether you're ordering 1,000 metres or 100,000 metres.

1. Width and Width Tolerance

Width is always listed first on any ribbon spec sheet. It's measured in millimetres (mm) and refers to the flat, unfolded width of the ribbon.

  • Common widths: 3mm, 6mm, 10mm, 16mm, 24mm, 38mm, 50mm, 75mm, 100mm
  • Tolerance: The ± figure tells you how much the actual width is allowed to deviate from the nominal. A spec of 25mm ±0.5mm means any width between 24.5mm and 25.5mm is acceptable.

⚠️ Why Width Tolerance Matters for Your Brand

A tolerance that's too wide (±2mm on a 16mm ribbon) can cause major problems in automated bow-tying machines or when the ribbon is intended for a specific packaging slot. Always match your tolerance to your application — retail gift packaging typically requires ±0.3mm to ±0.5mm.

2. Thickness and GSM (Grammage)

Thickness is measured in two ways:

  • Microns (µm): Physical thickness of a single layer of fabric. Typical ranges: 0.05mm to 0.25mm depending on material.
  • GSM (g/m²): Grams per square metre — the weight of one square metre of ribbon material. This is the most important indicator of perceived quality and body.

3. Material Composition

The material field specifies the fibre content of the ribbon. This affects everything from washability and fragrance resistance to OEKO-TEX eligibility and cost.

Common materials and their trade names:

  • Polyester (PET): Most common. Durable, colourfast, printable. Standard satin and grosgrain.
  • Nylon: Stronger and more elastic than polyester. Common in hair bows and stretch applications.
  • Silk: Premium. Rare in volume B2B orders due to cost — more common in luxury fashion and fine gift packaging.
  • Cotton: Breathable, biodegradable. Less common in high-volume decorative ribbon but growing in sustainable lines.
  • RPET (Recycled PET): Made from post-consumer plastic bottles. GRS-certified option for ESG-focused brands.
  • Bamboo Fibre: Emerging sustainable option with natural antibacterial properties.
Tip: If your brand has OEKO-TEX STANDARD 100 or GRS certification requirements, confirm the material composition upfront. Polyester and nylon are both certifiable; cotton and bamboo require separate organic certification chains.

4. Weave / Construction Type

How the ribbon is woven determines its texture, durability, and visual character. The most common weave types:

  • Plain weave: Simple over-under structure. Used for most printed ribbons and many satin ribbons.
  • Twill weave: Diagonal texture visible on the surface. Grosgrain ribbons typically use a variant of twill.
  • Satin weave: Floating yarns on the surface create the characteristic high-shine face. The underside remains duller.
  • Jacquard weave: Complex patterns woven directly into the fabric. No printing required — the design is structural.
  • Terry weave: Looped pile surface. Common in velvet ribbons.

5. Pantone Colour Matching

This is often where quality分歧 occurs between buyers and suppliers. The spec should reference a specific Pantone chip (e.g., Pantone 186 C) rather than a vague description like "red."

Key questions to ask your supplier:

  • Are you matching to CMYK process values, or to a physical Pantone chip?
  • What is the acceptable Delta-E (ΔE) tolerance? Industry standard for textile printing is ΔE ≤ 4.0 for solid colours.
  • Is the match based on a physical swatch or a digital reference (PDF/AI file)?
⚠️ Digital-to-print colour mismatch is the #1 cause of rejection in custom ribbon orders. A design file on screen rarely matches printed output. Always request a pre-production physical sample (lab dip or strike-off) before mass production — especially for brand-critical colours like logo reds, navy blues, or metallic golds.

7. Finishing and Edge Treatment

The edge finish affects how the ribbon drapes, whether it frays, and whether it's compatible with high-speed cutting and bow-tying equipment:

  • Heat-cut / Ultrasonic-cut: Edges are melted to prevent fraying. Cleanest finish for synthetic ribbons. Standard for polyester satin and grosgrain.
  • Mercerised edge: Cotton ribbon treated to prevent fraying while preserving a soft feel.
  • Pinked edge: Zigzag edge — mostly decorative, less common in B2B.
  • Woven selvedge edge: Self-finishing edge on jacquard and narrow fabrics.
  • Merrow edge: Overlocked stitch edge. Common on organza ribbons used in decorative applications.

8. Roll Specifications

Roll length and core size affect how your production line feeds the ribbon and how much waste you tolerate in start-up and wind-off:

  • Standard roll length: 45m, 50m, 91m — dependent on width and material.
  • Core size: Usually 1 inch (25.4mm) cardboard or plastic core. Confirm with your packaging team — automatic dispensers may require 3-inch cores.
  • Maximum roll diameter: Usually 10–12cm. Larger rolls may cause feeding problems in narrow-space machinery.

9. Shrinkage and Wash Stability

Often overlooked, shrinkage tolerance is critical if your ribbon will be used in products that are washed or exposed to heat:

  • Standard polyester: Typical shrinkage ≤ 3% after one wash at 40°C.
  • Pre-shrunk or heat-set: ≤ 1% — important for apparel-adjacent applications.
  • OEKO-TEX testing: Certified ribbons undergo specific wash-fastness tests. Request test reports for any ribbon used in children's products or apparel.

10. Defect Tolerances and AQL

A quality spec sheet should reference acceptable defect rates, typically expressed as an AQL (Acceptable Quality Level):

  • AQL 2.5 (general inspection): Standard for most decorative ribbon applications.
  • AQL 1.0 (tight inspection): For brand-critical applications — visible logo ribbons, colour-critical packaging.
  • Common defects monitored: Colour variation, width out of tolerance, weaving flaws, print registration errors, selvage defects, splice marks.

✅ What to Include When Sending Your Own Spec Sheet to a Supplier

When requesting a quote, include as many of the following fields as possible:

  1. Nominal width + tolerance (e.g., 25mm ±0.5mm)
  2. GSM / thickness target
  3. Material + any certification requirements (OEKO-TEX, GRS, etc.)
  4. Pantone reference for each colour + CMYK values
  5. Print method (if pre-decided)
  6. Quantity required per colour/pattern (MOQ applies)
  7. Roll length and core size
  8. Finishing requirements (heat-cut, ultrasonic, merrow)
  9. Shrinkage / wash-fastness requirements
  10. AQL target for quality inspection

Final Checklist: Before You Approve Any Ribbon Quote

Run through this checklist before confirming a purchase order with any ribbon supplier:

  • ☐ Width tolerance is tight enough for your packaging machinery or manual application
  • ☐ GSM is appropriate for the ribbon's end-use (structural vs decorative)
  • ☐ Pantone matching reference is a physical chip, not just a hex code
  • ☐ Pre-production sample approved for brand-critical colours
  • ☐ Print method confirmed for your artwork complexity
  • ☐ OEKO-TEX or GRS certificate requested if required by your brand standards
  • ☐ Roll length and core size confirmed for your production line
  • ☐ AQL standard specified in the purchase order
  • ☐ Lead time accounts for sample approval + production + shipping
  • ☐ Incoterms and destination clearly defined (DAP/DDP preferred for simplicity)
